Between Earth and Sky, Romania and the Pacific

First exhibited at the Embassy of Romania in Canberra, PacificaRomania gathers over 150 works — painted Dreamings, war canoes, carved ancestors, and sacred vessels from across Oceania and Island Southeast Asia — read through the comparative history of religion of Mircea Eliade and the sculptural cosmology of Constantin Brâncuși.

A Curatorial Lens

Why Romania looks at the Pacific

The collection is built on a dialogue between two traditions of thought about the sacred — one Romanian, one Indigenous Pacific — that arrive at strikingly similar conclusions.

Mircea Eliade

The Romanian historian of religion gave the collection its working vocabulary: the axis mundi, the hierophany, the eternal return. His comparative method — reading Aboriginal Dreaming, Bornean shamanism, and Melanesian ritual as expressions of one deep human grammar — is the collection's interpretive spine.

Constantin Brâncuși

Brâncuși's Bird in Space and his lifelong pursuit of ascent, threshold, and essential form echo through Arnhem Land poles and totemic carvings alike. He is the collection's aesthetic counterpoint — modernism arriving, independently, at ancient conclusions.

Living Traditions

These are not relics. Mah Meri carvers, Batak ritual specialists, and Aboriginal painters continue these practices today. The collection treats each object as a living hierophany, not a closed chapter.
