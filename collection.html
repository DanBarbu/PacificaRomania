Organised by region. Each object is presented as the collection's own curatorial notes describe it, alongside further reading from museums and specialists where relevant.

Region

Australia — Dreaming & the Desert Sky

Seven Sisters Dreaming

Gabriella Possum Nungurrayi · Dot painting

The Kungkarangkalpa (Seven Sisters) Dreaming recounts the pursuit of ancestral women across the land and into the sky, immortalised in dot paintings where concentric circles and U-shapes mark sacred sites and the sisters' movements. The story encodes navigation knowledge, seasonal cycles, and social law for Western Desert peoples, and finds a striking, independently-evolved echo in the Greek myth of the Pleiades.

Read the curatorial essay → · Japingka Aboriginal Art

My Country, Water Dreaming

Marlene Young Nungurrayi · Dot painting

A vast topographical map of life-giving water systems and ancestral tracks across the desert, rendered in networks of blue and white dots punctuated by yellow concentric circles marking sites of mana and abundance.

Yolngu Hollow Log Coffins & Pukumani Poles

Arnhem Land · Painted & carved wood installation

Towering mortuary poles that function as an axis mundi, connecting the terrestrial world to the celestial realm of the ancestors — a threshold ensuring the soul's safe passage, installed here in dialogue with a monumental Seven Sisters painting and Constantin Brâncuși's sculptural ascent.

Read the curatorial essay → · NGA: Painted Objects from Arnhem Land

Papua New Guinea & the Solomon Islands — Spirits of Prow and Highland

Asaro Spirit Mask

Eastern Highlands, Papua New Guinea · Clay & fibre

A pale, hollow-eyed mask in the tradition of the Asaro Mudmen (Holosa), whose river-mud disguise once turned a retreat into legend: warriors emerging from the Asaro River at dusk, caked in grey mud, were mistaken for vengeful spirits by a hostile tribe — a defence the Asaro deliberately preserved in mask form ever after.

Read the curatorial essay →

Nguzu Nguzu

Western Province, Solomon Islands · Carved wood, nautilus shell inlay (revareva)

Lashed to the waterline prow of a war canoe, this protective spirit warded off Kesoko (water fiends) and ensured calm seas. Its cultural gravity was such that the Nguzu Nguzu passed from tribal talisman to national symbol, appearing today on Solomon Islands coinage.

Read the curatorial essay → · Smarthistory

Tomoko

Solomon Islands · Carved war canoe with rowers

The primary instrument of the pre-colonial Pacific war economy, in which the taking of a head was a spiritual requirement for "powering" a new canoe or communal house — and in which captured children were frequently adopted into the victor's family. Reimagined today as the ceremonial "Peace Canoe."

Vanuatu & Fiji — The Pacific Way in Colour

Nautical Rhythms, Tautahi & Tamahina, Fidji

Aloï Pilioko · Painting

Called the "Picasso of the Pacific," Pilioko — born in Wallis and Futuna and rooted in Vanuatu and Fiji — bridges Polynesian tradition with modern expressionism: azure waters and gold for the sea's mana, sacred reds and ethereal whites for the totemic rooster, and a jungle palette of floral crowns and watchful ancestral eyes.

Borneo, Sumatra & the Malay Peninsula — Guardians of the Threshold

Papot (Baby Carrier)

Dayak, Borneo · Carved wood, rattan, beadwork

Far more than a means of transport, the papot is a portable sacred centre — a spiritual shield carved with fierce guardian faces, serpentine motifs, and hornbill wings, invoking ancestral spirits to ward off malevolent forces and carry an infant safely through both jungle and threshold.

Read the curatorial essay →

Naga Morsarang

Batak, Sumatra · Carved buffalo horn medicine container

A hollow buffalo horn holding ritual medicine (pupuk), animated by a carved stopper of ancestral or hybrid figures — in its most elaborate form a complete cosmogram uniting horn, serpent-dragon, and lineage of ancestors into one operative instrument for navigating illness and the unseen.

Read the curatorial essay → · Indonesia Museum

Moyang Siamang

Jah Hut or Mah Meri, Peninsular Malaysia · Carved hardwood

A carved ancestor-spirit figure of the primate siamang, one of more than 460 documented moyang spirit forms in the animist cosmological atlas of the Orang Asli — the original peoples of the Malay Peninsula, for whom the gibbon's dawn call marks the threshold between the human and the more-than-human world.

Read the curatorial essay →

Mah Meri Ancestral Masks

Mah Meri (Senoi), Selangor, Malaysia · Carved wood

Masks that materialise the Moyang (ancestor spirits), carved by shamans said to be spontaneously "chosen" through dream — the Mah Meri rituals that Mircea Eliade himself drew on in Shamanism as an example of the shaman as psychopomp, bridge between the living and the dead.

Myanmar — The Sacralization of Measure

Chinthe Master Weight

Burma, 18th–19th century · Bronze

A majestic lion-gryphon "master weight," sentinel of the marketplace axis mundi and guardian of the pagoda, representing the solar and fire element in a merchant's set of scales that quietly unites Heaven, Earth, and Underworld.

Read the curatorial essay →

Elephant & Fish Weights

Burma, 18th–19th century · Bronze

Elephant weights ground the set in the Earth element; fish and dolphin weights, rarer still, evoke the fluid abundance of the primordial waters — together with the Chinthe, a complete cosmology cast in bronze for daily trade.
