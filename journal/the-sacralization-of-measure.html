Myanmar

The Sacralization of Measure

Burmese weights in the PacificaRomania collection.

Historic Burmese metrology beautifully blends state commerce with profound religious symbolism. The collection features a scientifically significant set of animal-shaped bronze weights, each carrying meaning far beyond the marketplace.

At the centre is the massive “master weight” Chinthe (lion-gryphon), a majestic guardian representing the solar and fire element and acting as a sentinel of the axis mundi. It is supported by precise elephant weights grounding the earth element, and by extremely rare fish and dolphin weights symbolising the fluid abundance of the primordial waters.

Together on the merchant’s scale, these eighteenth- and nineteenth-century artifacts unite the three realms of the cosmos — heaven, earth, and underworld — into a single tool, ensuring moral integrity and sacred law in daily trade.

Echoes Across the Collection

The Chinthe’s role as guardian of the pagoda and sentinel of the marketplace threshold echoes the spiritual function of the pale, hollow-eyed Asaro spirit mask of Papua New Guinea elsewhere in the collection. Whether an imposing, roaring lion cast in bronze or a ghostly clay spectre, both objects use an otherworldly visage to protect a critical physical and spiritual boundary.

The set’s cosmological mapping — Chinthe as heaven, elephant as earth, fish as underworld — is mirrored in the collection’s Australian installation, where towering mortuary poles connect the terrestrial world to the celestial realm of the ancestors, and a great blue-and-white water-dreaming painting maps the aquatic element beneath the desert. Whether cast in high-tin bronze, moulded from river clay, or painted on canvas and ironwood, these objects serve to anchor the sacred directly into the material world.
